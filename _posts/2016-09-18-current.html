--- layout: post title: Design, Dieter Rams and the Spoon description: Dieter Rams design principles applied to the spoon. ---

Solving a design problem usually requires a trail and error approach involving many attempts at a solution. Each attempt gets closer to a specification which has been set by the demands of a customer. However, most designs are a compromise between its function, aesthetics and cost. The compromise always raises the question as to how 'good' the solution to the design problem actually is.

Faced with the same problem Dieter Rams, in the late 70's, asked himself if his designs were good designs. In answering his question he developed ten important principles for what could be considered a good design.

To explore the design principles I have taken what may appear to be the trivial example of a spoon. However, it is an object that has been used for many centuries and its evolution is a good test of its continuing use. The design of the spoon consists of a bowl and handle and is mainly used to prepare ,serve and eat food. The earliest known spoons were used by the ancient Greeks and were composed of ivory, flint, slate or wood and possibly used in religious ceremonies. Today we are never far away from a spoon.

Taking the design of a spoon and assessing it against Rams' design principles we get:

Good design is innovative - The possibilities for innovation are not, by any means, exhausted. Technological development is always offering new opportunities for innovative design. But innovative design always develops in tandem with innovative technology, and can never be an end in itself.

There have been many innovations in materials over the years which have found their way into the design of a spoon. In recent times plastic is used for disposable spoons which can be found on ice cream tubs. These innovations have brought advances in the use of the spoon e.g. wooden spoons are more ecologically friendly. Maybe in the future sensing devices may be included in a spoon to help monitor peoples eating habits or ensuring people with dementia have eaten.

Good design makes a product useful - A product is bought to be used. It has to satisfy certain criteria, not only functional, but also psychological and aesthetic. Good design emphasis the usefulness of a product whilst disregarding anything that could possibly detract from it.

A spoon is integral to our way of life and is taken for granted - until we can't find one ! Also, we use a spoon without thinking about its use - when was the last time you concentrated on using a spoon when eating sticky toffee pudding ?

Good design is aesthetic - The aesthetic quality of a product is integral to its usefulness because products we use every day affect our person and our well-being. But only well-executed objects can be beautiful.

The early history of the spoon was seen as a social status but because of mass manufacturing it has become common place. If a spoon becomes over decorated then it can be unbalanced, or heavy, which can distract in its use.

Good design makes a product understandable - It clarifies the product’s structure. Better still, it can make the product talk. At best, it is self-explanatory.

The design of a spoon - the bowl and handle - is clear in its use.

Good design is unobtrusive - Products fulfilling a purpose are like tools. They are neither decorative objects nor works of art. Their design should therefore be both neutral and restrained, to leave room for the user’s self-expression.

A spoon can go beyond its normal use but it is only if the user should desire. For example it can be used to demonstrate the principles of levers, or used as a pointer, the handle can be used as a bicycle tyre lever or to propel a pea through the air ! The key point is that it is down to the user to express its use.

Good design is honest - It does not make a product more innovative, powerful or valuable than it really is. It does not attempt to manipulate the consumer with promises that cannot be kept.

The shape of design of a spoon does not go beyond its function. It can't drill a hole or hammer in a nail or cut cloth.

Good design is long-lasting - It avoids being fashionable and therefore never appears antiquated. Unlike fashionable design, it lasts many years – even in today’s throwaway society.

Although the ornamental design of a spoon has changed over the centuries to reflect its social status its underlying shape has stayed the same.

Good design is thorough down to the last detail - Nothing must be arbitrary or left to chance. Care and accuracy in the design process show respect towards the user.

The design of a spoon has no additional frills. It sticks to its function.

Good design is environmentally-friendly - Design makes an important contribution to the preservation of the environment. It conserves resources and minimize physical and visual pollution throughout the lifecycle of the product.

Most of the metal used in manufacturing a spoon can be recycled. Similarly for wooden spoons supplied with ice cream tubs.

Rams design principles highlight features of the spoon that have stood the test of time and its is an example of a 'good' design. I am sure that applied to similar objects.

However an interesting question about Rams design principles is whether they can be applied to a service or process. If not maybe there is scope for a new set of design principles with the same flavour as Dieter Rams'.

Quotes extracted from Vitsoe, Dieter Rams: ten principles for good design
