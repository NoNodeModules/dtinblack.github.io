--- layout: post title: Listening Between The Notes description: Listening to sound with Stockhausen. ---

For a long time I didn’t understand Stockhausen. His music was full of squeaks, trickles, crunches and sometimes long periods of silence. Every year I would sit down and listen very hard, but after five minutes I would give up and listen to something else. For somebody who grew up on Jimi Hendrix, The Who and Led Zeppelin it was a million miles from a driving beat or soaring lead guitar. I was aware of classic music but my taste fell under the category of ‘popular classic’. However, a few years ago I sat down for my yearly attempt to understand Stockhausen. After a few minutes I decided to turn the volume up; a door into his music creaked open. For me, Stockhausen is exploring the colour of sound. There is more to music than the rhythm, harmony and melody. Stockhausen was exploring the richness of sound that we would normally dismiss as ‘noise’. By concentrating on the sound between the notes, I could hear a deeper source of its energy and emotion.

From then on my music collection took on a different significance. I started to appreciate a wider range of classic music and in particular piano music. I suddenly found the joy in the piano playing of the Grunshlag Sisters, and John Adams music opened up new dimensions., and my old favourites such as Beethoven took on a new freshness. However, returning to my early influences rock music my new appreciation of sound changed the way that I heard the playing of the musicians that are hidden in the background: John Bonham, Keith Moon, and John Entwistle brought richness to the music of their bands. Also, listening to the wider sound in music has confirmed the iconic status of albums such as Captain Beafheart’s Trout Mask Replica!.

Many unusual sources have found their way onto my listening list, for example the Navajo Indians Night Chant that is both haunting and sounds spiritual. But the sound between the notes is the big message, which I continue to take from Stockhausen’s music. This approach can be applied to areas outside of music: walking through a city of the countryside opens up new sounds; no longer does bird song fill the countryside but the wind rattling through the leaves and the cattle rasping the grass is enlightening. Even the distance noise of traffic with a police siren-bleeping cutting through the cacophony brings richness to the experience. Also, new ‘artists’ have been added to my list for example Chris Watson who captures everyday sounds in a way that exposes its richness. And the resonance of the human voice takes on a greater quality and can be heard in programs such as BBC Radio 3 Between The Ears and BBC Radio 4 The Listening Project

Wittgenstein wrote that "people nowadays think that scientists exist to instruct them, poets, musicians, etc to give them pleasure. The idea that these have something to teach them – that does not occur to them". My education started when I turned up the music and listened to the sound in between the notes.
