Like bubbles of air that burst through the surface of a pond in a warm summers day, Bayes Theorem pops up across the many pools that make up the media. Newspaper articles, blogs and scientific magazines try valiantly to describe the impact of Bayes Theorem on everyday life with a few terms from mathematics tentatively pushed around.

Reading through the media it seems that Bayes Theorem is applied to a diverse range of areas: assessing skill in computer games, developing medical devices, filtering out spam including the now legendary bank account in Nigeria, search patterns for finding lost planes, assessing which advert will pop up when using your favourite search engine, and playing a crucial role in the “war on terrorisim”. Bayes Theorem is linked with Artificial Intelligence, including how machines can learn, a situation that provoked Prof. Stephen Hawkins to pronounce dire warnings about the future of humanity, or the lack of it ! Bayes Theorem seems to have crept into our way of life. But what is it ?

Trawling across the web gathers up articles with titles such as: "Belief, bias and Bayes", "The Odds, Continually Updated", and Tomb with a grave view. However, the articles soon diverge onto the controversies between “Bayesians and Frequentists” or through around the mysterious term: “Inverse Probability”. Where there are claims of simple explanations many of the diagrams look like a map of a battle field: full of squiggles and strange symbols, or descriptions using boxes with coloured balls, spinning coins or the chances of developing cancer. Against this background, subsequent posts will attempt to give a clearer picture of Bayes Theorem, and hopefully clear away some of the dense fog that surrounds it.

Information about the Rev. Thomas Bayes is vague, but what we know is that he was born sometime in 1701 and was probably born in Hertfordshire. He studied Theology and Logic and Edinburgh University as apart of his ordination to become a Nonconformist Minister. He took up his ministry at the Moint Sion Chapel in Tunbridge Wells until 1752. He died 1761 and was buried in Moorgate, London.

The Rev. Bayes had a keen interest in mathematics and published two papers in his lifetime. He was elected as a Fellow of the Royal Society in 1742 probably based on one of the papers, which was a logical defense of the mathematical techniques developed by Newton. In his later years he took a deeper interest in probability, which may have been influenced by a book he read on chance in gambling.

It is unclear when Bayes wrote the unpublished paper that would include the theorem that his name would becomme firmly attached too, but it was found after his death by his friend Richard Price, who sent it to the Royal Society and where it was read in 1763.

What was the problem Bayes was trying to solve ? Why was it important ? Why does it have such a grip on our lives ? The next post will start to uncover the some answers.
