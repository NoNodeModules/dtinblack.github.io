--- layout: post title: Beyond The Limits of On-line Learning description: Thoughts about the future of on-line learning. ---

Developments in web technology has opened up access to education beyond the classroom, for example Massive Online Open Access ( MOOC ). MOOCs are available to people around the world at any time of the day, or night, and cover a wide range of subjects. However, will the web ever be able to deliver the ideal learning situation: one-to-one ?

Based on the MOOCs I have undertaken the quality of the teaching is very varied. Competence assessment is conducted by quizzes, or submitting material for assessment, at the end of each section of the course. Access to the expert is limited to their comments on forums where participants are encouraged to discuss what they have learned or problems that they have experienced. The delivery costs are low and I suspect that the course development costs are minimal because they appear to be using course material that would have been delivered traditionally ( for those developing MOOCs can I suggest Derek Rowntree’s excellent book Teaching Through Self-Instruction: How to Develop Open Learning Materials for the design of distance learning material ). Also, there is no alignment or assessment of participants competence about a subject before starting a course or their ability in ‘learning how to learn’ which is important for on-line learning.

Here are some thoughts about improving on-line learning:

1 ) On-line learning is limited to subjects that rely heavily on text, video and graphics. However there are many subjects where learning is achieved by doing fro example many of the craft and trades. Could a system be developed to monitor and advise on learning-by-doing ? Lets take an example: hammering a nail into a piece of wood. A web based system could monitor ( something like Kinect ? ) the swing of the hammer, the point where the nail was struck etc. and then offer advice on how to improve the hammering technique. This is a simple example to highlight the principles however it could be extended to other learning-by-doing activities.

2 ) We often learn more about a subject through making mistakes than correctly answering questions about it. One of the problems with the current on-line learning systems is that the learner gets no feedback on where they have gone wrong if they incorrectly answer a question. If a system could gather the steps that the learner made in arriving at an answer then it could work out where they had gone wrong and provide advice ( could development of Q-Learning type algorithm be used ?)

3 ) The Web is full of information that includes lecture notes, Wikipedia, blogs etc. However the material implicitly assumes a level of competency about the reader. A system that logged the readers level of competency on subjects could match the search results to their competency ( some form of competence assessment similar to that used by Knewton and integrated with Semantic Web technology ? ).

One-to-one learning is an ideal situation but currently cost prohibitive. Can further development of web based technology open up learning beyond the classroom and achieve one-to-one learning ?
