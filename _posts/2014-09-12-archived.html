--- layout: post title: The Crisp Packet Challenge description: Forecasting health conditions ---

Every Friday at around 9 o'clock the fridge door is swung open and bottles of beer are popped open, wine glasses filled and crisps are poured into large bowls that are surrounded with a selection of dips. It is family ritual. However we can't claim originality. It started many years ago after watching a documentary about the adventures of Sir Chey Blythe. No mater where he was in the world, between 6 and 7 in the evening, he and the crew would stop whatever they were doing and drink a few glasses of whiskey. It was a moment in a dangerous and hectic life when they could stop, take in the fantastic scenery, and put life back into some sort of perspective. What was good for Sir Chey is good for us. Our ritual has developed into a period when we can come together as a family, catch up, and steady the ship after the usual stormy week.

However, during the last few years like a head win that has sprung out of nowhere the 'H' word started to creep into our family ritual. Gusts of : 'Is this good for our health ?','What impact is this doing to our health ?' and 'Shouldn't we have less crisps?'. There is no doubt that our slug of beer-wine-crisps-dips is the top side of the recommended daily intake of calories but like a weather beaten sea dog I face resolutely into the ‘H’ead wind.

Medical progress is accelerating, and exciting new discoveries are announced every week. The dropping price of technology has allowed the development of "fitness bands" and other wearable sensors that can measure vital signs. All this and the vast amount of medical information available on the web have contributed to improving health. But one of the biggest impacts on health remains lifestyle, and in particular diet. In the media there seems to be what can be considered as generalisations. Statements such as "Eat a balanced diet" – but what is the balance ?, Or the see sawing between "red wine is okay", "red win is not okay" or "too much butter is bad for you", "but some butter is okay" and so it goes on.

However it seems to be difficult, if not impossible, to accurately predict an individual's health from their diet, and more importantly on how long we will live. Therefore I have developed The Crisp Bag Challenge, which is, if I eat another bag of crisps during our family get together how many hours, minutes or even seconds will it reduce my life span? Of course I offer no prizes. However, the winner, or winners, can glow in the knowledge that you will have resolved at least one family dispute around a fridge. In the meantime we will still be looking forward to our Friday night ‘Happy Hour’, imaging Sir Chey Blyth, glass in hand, out on the open seas.
